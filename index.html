Getting a New Role Live

A step-by-step guide to publishing roles in First for Greenhouse users.

First screens every applicant against your criteria automatically ‚Äî so your team spends time interviewing, not sifting through CVs.

This guide walks through the three steps to get a role live: syncing roles from Greenhouse, configuring your AI screening criteria, and reviewing candidates and refining criteria.

Step 1. Create your role in Greenhouse

Create your role in Greenhouse as usual. When you do, it will automatically be imported into First ‚Äî no manual import needed.

A role will go live on the careers site when two conditions are met: the job is open, and it has an external job post set to live. To unpublish a role, just change either of those things in Greenhouse.

The role will then appear in First. Now click Add Criteria on the role to go straight into the setup page where you'll configure the application process.

Step 2. Configure your screening criteria & questions

This is where you set up the criteria that every candidate is assessed against ‚Äî requirements, skills, experience, and screening questions. Everything in step 2 happens within the Application Process page shown here, so this is the only screen you need to work with.

Using the AI assistant to set up roles

Instead of configuring everything manually, the AI Assistant in Role Settings can read your job description and generate criteria and questions for you.

Use it from the start to get a solid foundation, or fire it up later to make changes to an existing setup. You can have a back and forth with it to sense check adjustments before committing.

Tip
If there's criteria you're looking for that isn't in the job description, you can chat with the AI to get it added and sense check it. Try asking: "Besides what's on the spec, is there anything else I should be looking for?"

Check Skills and Experience

This is where you define the experience and expertise the role requires. The AI reads each candidate's CV and evaluates them against the criteria you set. Each criterion has a priority level that determines how it affects scoring.

üíº Past Roles

Checks whether a candidate has experience in specific roles. This isn't a keyword match ‚Äî the AI infers relevant experience, including similar and equivalent job titles.

Define the role: Type the job title you're looking for. Use the suggest function to surface common titles or functional areas ‚Äî the AI can also suggest similar titles to broaden or narrow your search.

Set minimum experience: Choose the minimum time in this type of role, in years or months. Set to 0 if you don't mind how long ‚Äî the AI will still check for relevant experience, just without a minimum threshold.

Set a priority level: Reject if not met, Required to shortlist, or Preferred.

You can add multiple past role criteria per role ‚Äî e.g. one required and one preferred.

The Check Past Roles screen ‚Äî add job titles, set minimum experience, and choose a priority level.
üèóÔ∏è Past Companies

Checks whether a candidate has worked at specific companies. Enter the company names you want to see on their CV ‚Äî the AI checks their work history for matches.

Set a priority level: Reject if not met, Required to shortlist, or Preferred.

The Check Past Companies screen ‚Äî add company names and set importance level.
üè≠ Industry Experience

Checks whether a candidate has worked in specific industries or sectors. Unlike Past Companies, this is about the industry, not the employer.

Add industries: Add one or more industries relevant to the role. Each is a separate criterion with its own importance level ‚Äî e.g. require Automotive, prefer Robotics.

Set minimum time: Optionally set a minimum in years or months ‚Äî e.g. "1+ year in Automotive".

Set a priority level: Reject if not met, Required to shortlist, or Preferred.

The Check Industry Experience screen ‚Äî add industries, set importance and minimum time per industry.
üîß Skills and Keywords

Checks a candidate's CV for specific skills, tools, languages, frameworks, or methodologies. Skills are organised into groups ‚Äî you can have as many groups as you need.

Create a group: Add the skills you want to check for. For example, one group might be "Python, Machine Learning, Statistical analysis" and another "MLflow, Airflow, Spark".

AND vs OR matching: For each group, choose how skills are matched:

OR ‚Äî candidate needs at least one from the group. Use when skills are interchangeable, e.g. "Python OR R OR Julia".

AND ‚Äî candidate needs every skill in the group. Use when all are genuinely required together.

Note: Think carefully about what you group together. A broad OR group like "Python OR Excel OR SQL" would match a data engineer and an office administrator equally ‚Äî if those candidates aren't interchangeable for the role, they probably shouldn't be in the same group.

Set a priority level: Reject if not met, Required to shortlist, or Preferred.

The Check Skills and Keywords screen ‚Äî add skill groups, choose AND/OR matching, and set importance.
üéì Education and Training

Checks whether a candidate has specific qualifications or training. You can add multiple, each with its own importance level.

Subject or programme: What the qualification is in ‚Äî e.g. "Computer Science", "Data Engineering", or "CIPD Level 5".

Qualification type: Select the level ‚Äî Bachelor's degree, Master's degree, PhD, Certification, Training, or Bootcamp.

Additional context: Optional free-text for specifics ‚Äî e.g. "from a Russell Group university" or "must include a placement year".

Set a priority level: Reject if not met, Required to shortlist, or Preferred.

The Check Education and Training screen ‚Äî add qualifications, select type, and set importance.
‚úçÔ∏è Custom Prompt

Title: A short label for this criterion ‚Äî e.g. "Production embedded systems" or "Platform migration leadership".

Prompt: Describe the experience you want evidence of. Be specific ‚Äî e.g. "Experience building and deploying autonomous vehicle software in production" or "Has led a team through a major platform migration".

Set a priority level: Reject if not met, Required to shortlist, or Preferred.

You can add multiple custom prompts, each checking for something different with its own priority level.

The Custom Prompt screen ‚Äî add a title, describe the experience you're looking for, and set a priority level.

Test Knowledge and Behaviour

Screening questions that go beyond what's on a CV. These test how a candidate thinks, how they approach problems, and whether they have the depth the role needs. You add open-ended questions that candidates answer in their own words as part of the application. The AI evaluates each response against the intent of your question and factors it into the candidate's overall score.

We'd recommend 2 to 3 questions per role. That's enough to get meaningful signal without making the application too long. Think about the questions you'd normally ask at phone screen or first interview ‚Äî and move them here instead.

‚öôÔ∏è Technical Knowledge

Test for specific technical depth. First you'll choose a format:

üí¨ AI Interview
Conversational, AI-led interview on hard skills

A conversational, AI-led interview on hard skills. The candidate answers your question, and the AI follows up to probe deeper ‚Äî similar to a live phone screen, but automated.

When you set up an AI Interview, you'll fill in four areas:

1. What do you want to test: Describe the skills you want the AI interviewer to assess. This gives the AI context on what to probe for ‚Äî e.g. "real-time systems design" or "data pipeline architecture".

2. Select a question: The AI generates interview questions based on the job spec and the context you provided. Pick one, or write your own if you'd prefer something more specific.

3. Prompt guidance: Three fields that shape how the interview is scored:

Candidate guidance ‚Äî context the AI can share with the candidate to help them answer accurately.

Acceptable answer ‚Äî describe what a satisfactory response looks like, so the AI knows the minimum bar.

Great answer ‚Äî a customisable scoring rubric that defines what an outstanding response looks like.

4. Advanced settings: This is where you set the priority level ‚Äî Preferred, Required to shortlist, or Reject if not met.

üìù Review Code Snippet
Candidate reviews code and identifies issues

The candidate reviews a code snippet and identifies issues. The AI evaluates their response for technical understanding, not just whether they spotted a specific bug. Useful for roles where reading and reasoning about code matters as much as writing it.

1. Skills to test: Pick a skill for the code review ‚Äî e.g. "Python", "React", or "SQL".

2. Difficulty: Choose a level ‚Äî Junior, Intermediate, or Senior. This controls the complexity of the code snippet generated.

3. Review and edit: Hit Generate and the AI will create the question. You can enable strict mode, which tracks tab switches, copy and paste, and other activity during the review.

4. Great answer criteria: Describe what a great answer looks like ‚Äî this tells the AI what to reward when scoring responses.

5. Snippet and answers: Fine-tune any details around the question, code snippet, or expected answers.

6. Advanced settings: Set the priority level ‚Äî Preferred, Required to shortlist, or Reject if not met.

ü§ù Behaviours and Questions

Understand how someone works in practice ‚Äî their problem-solving style, communication, and approach under pressure.

üí¨ Behavioural questions
Open-ended questions on problem-solving, communication, and approach

Open-ended questions that reveal how a candidate thinks and operates ‚Äî same flow as AI Interview. For example: "Share an experience where you worked with unclear or incomplete requirements. How did you approach the situation and ensure successful delivery?"

1. What do you want to test: Describe the behaviours or soft skills you want the AI to assess ‚Äî e.g. "collaboration under pressure" or "stakeholder management".

2. Select a question: The AI generates questions based on the job spec and context you provided. Pick one, or write your own.

3. Prompt guidance: Three fields that shape how the interview is scored:

Candidate guidance ‚Äî context the AI can share with the candidate to help them answer accurately.

Acceptable answer ‚Äî describe what a satisfactory response looks like, so the AI knows the minimum bar.

Great answer ‚Äî describe what an outstanding response looks like.

4. Advanced settings: Set the priority level ‚Äî Preferred, Required to shortlist, or Reject if not met.

Tip
The best screening questions are ones where a strong candidate's answer will sound noticeably different to a weak candidate's. Avoid questions with a single correct answer ‚Äî those don't give the AI much to differentiate on.
Step 3. Review candidates and refine criteria

Candidates who apply are screened against everything you've configured ‚Äî requirements, skills, and question responses. Each candidate receives a score and is sorted automatically:

Shortlist ‚Äî candidates who meet all your required-to-shortlist criteria are pushed straight into the shortlist.
Inbox ‚Äî everyone else appears in an ordered list, ranked from strongest to weakest.
Not a fit ‚Äî anyone missing minimum requirements is flagged as not a fit.

From there, your candidates are ready for you to manage and prioritise your time with the strongest applicants first. All actions sync back to Greenhouse automatically.

When you shortlist a candidate, they're pushed through to Phone Screen in Greenhouse. When you reject a candidate, they're rejected in Greenhouse too.

Any changes you make to your criteria can reassess all existing candidates, so rankings always reflect the latest setup. Use this to refine as you go.
